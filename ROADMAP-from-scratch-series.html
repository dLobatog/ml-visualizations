From Scratch Series

Build-up educational narratives: each visualization adds exactly one concept, constructing complete systems step by step

Roadmap & Wireframes • 7 Chapters • ~55 Visualizations • For experts and newcomers alike

Design Philosophy

This series borrows the build-up narrative from Karpathy's "from scratch" lectures and adapts it to the interactive canvas format. The key difference from the existing interview-prep pages: instead of standalone visualizations that each explain one concept, every visualization in a chapter is a layer that builds on the previous one. The learner watches a complete working system assemble piece by piece.

🧱
Incremental Construction. Each viz adds exactly one new idea to the system built so far. The running system is always visible — new parts light up, old parts stay but are dimmed. A "minimap" at the top of the page shows where you are in the build.
🚨
"Break It" Toggles. Every major component can be disabled. Remove LayerNorm? Watch gradients explode. Zero out residuals? See training stall. Use sum instead of mean aggregation in a GNN? Watch node features blow up on high-degree nodes. This is how experts engage — they already know what attention is, but seeing it break in context deepens understanding.
🔍
Dual-Depth Content. Each viz has a newcomer layer (plain-language "what" + animated arrows) and an expert layer (KaTeX formulas, gradient flow analysis, complexity annotations). Toggle between them or show both.
🔗
Cross-Chapter Callbacks. When a concept from an earlier chapter appears, show a link/thumbnail back. "This attention mechanism? You built it in Ch3. Now we're putting it on graph edges (GAT)." Reinforces connections, makes the curriculum feel like a web rather than a list.
🎯
Live-Coded Feel. Where possible, show actual numbers flowing through. Not "here's the formula" — instead: "input = [0.2, -0.5, 0.8], W = [[...]], matmul = [...], ReLU = [...], loss = 2.34." The learner traces concrete values end-to-end.

Visualization 3.1

Weight Initialization: Why It Matters

Goal: Show that random init = dead neurons or exploding activations. Proper init (Xavier/He) fixes it.
canvas wireframe

Activation distribution histograms per layer. 10-layer MLP. With naive init (N(0,1)): activations saturate (all near 0 or 1). With Xavier: activations stay roughly N(0,1) at every layer. With He: same for ReLU.

Side-by-side: three columns for three init strategies. Each column shows a stack of 10 histograms (one per layer). The difference is visually striking.

Animated training: Watch what happens over 100 epochs. Bad init: loss barely decreases. Good init: loss drops quickly.
Visualization 3.2

Batch Normalization → Layer Normalization

Goal: Why normalize? Show internal covariate shift, then fix it. Then show why LN works better for sequences.
canvas wireframe

Three-panel: Activation distributions at layer 5 of a 10-layer MLP across training steps.

① No normalization: distribution shifts wildly between steps. ② BatchNorm: stable, centered at 0, std ~1. ③ LayerNorm: similar stability.

Key difference panel: Show what BN and LN normalize over. BN: normalizes across batch dimension (problematic for varying sequence lengths). LN: normalizes across feature dimension (works for any batch size, any sequence length).

"Break it": Remove normalization from a deep network → training diverges after a few epochs. Add it back → smooth training.
Visualization 3.3

Residual Connections: The Highway for Gradients

Goal: Show that skip connections let gradients flow directly through deep networks.
canvas wireframe

Gradient flow diagram: 20-layer network. Without residuals: gradient magnitude plot shows exponential decay. With residuals: gradient stays roughly constant across all layers.

Architecture toggle: Show the residual block: x + F(x). The "+" is the key — it provides a gradient highway (d/dx of x = 1, always). Even if F(x) has vanishing gradients, the shortcut carries the signal.

Depth experiment: Slider from 5 to 100 layers. Without residuals: accuracy degrades past ~20 layers. With residuals: accuracy keeps improving. Reproduces the core result from the ResNet paper.
Expert layer: Annotate the gradient path: ∇L = ∇L · (I + ∇F). The identity matrix I ensures gradient ≥ 1 along the skip path. This is why you can train 1000-layer networks with residuals.
Visualization 3.4

Optimizer Zoo: SGD → Momentum → Adam

Goal: Watch different optimizers navigate the same loss landscape. See why Adam is the default.
canvas wireframe

Contour plot of a pathological loss landscape (elongated valley, saddle point). Four colored balls, one per optimizer: SGD (red), SGD+Momentum (orange), RMSProp (blue), Adam (green).

All start at the same point. Watch their paths diverge. SGD oscillates across the valley. Momentum overshoots but converges faster. Adam navigates smoothly.

Internal state view: For Adam, show the running mean (m) and variance (v) for each parameter. Show how the adaptive LR is computed: LR * m / (√v + ε). Concrete numbers.
Visualization 3.5 — 3.6

Regularization: L2 & Dropout

Goal: Overfitting is visible. Watch regularization prevent it.
canvas wireframe

Viz 3.5 — L2/Weight Decay: Train/val loss curves. Without regularization: training loss hits 0, val loss diverges (overfitting). With L2: both converge. Show weight magnitude histogram shrinking with L2. "L2 penalty = preference for small weights = simpler model."

Viz 3.6 — Dropout: Network diagram where neurons randomly disappear during training (grayed out). Each forward pass uses a different random subset. "It's like training an ensemble of thin networks." At test time: all neurons active, weights scaled by (1-p).

Interactive: Dropout rate slider (0 to 0.9). At 0: overfits. At 0.5: good generalization. At 0.9: underfits (too much information destroyed).

Visualization 4.1

The Problem: Why MLPs Struggle with Sequences

Goal: Show that fixed-size context windows miss long-range dependencies. Motivate a new mechanism.
canvas wireframe

Example sentence: "The cat that sat on the mat by the door was ___"

Show an MLP with context=3 trying to predict "was." It only sees "the door ___" — not enough! It needs to know about "The cat" (8 tokens back). Highlight the dependency with an arc.

Show increasingly large context windows: 3, 5, 10, 20. Each time, more dependencies become reachable. But the model size grows quadratically with context. "We need a mechanism that can selectively look at any position."

Visualization 4.2

Attention as Soft Dictionary Lookup

Goal: Build intuition BEFORE the math. Attention = "soft" version of a dictionary/hashtable.
canvas wireframe

Left: Hard lookup. A Python dictionary: {"cat": "animal", "Paris": "city"}. Query "cat" → exact match → returns "animal". Query "kitten" → KeyError!

Right: Soft lookup. Same keys, but now query "kitten" returns a weighted blend: 0.7×"animal" + 0.2×"pet" + 0.1×"city". The weights come from how similar the query is to each key.

Animate: show similarity scores, softmax, weighted sum. "This is attention. The rest is just making the similarity scores learnable."

Interactive: User types a query word, sees it compared against all keys, watches the softmax weights form, sees the blended output. Change the query → different blend.
Visualization 4.3

Self-Attention: Q, K, V Projections

Goal: Show that Q, K, V are just learned linear projections that make the "soft lookup" trainable.
canvas wireframe

Sentence: "The cat sat." Each token starts as an embedding vector. Show the three projections: x × W_Q = query, x × W_K = key, x × W_V = value.

For the token "cat": its query says "I'm looking for subjects/verbs related to me." Its key says "I'm an animal noun." Its value says "Here's my semantic content."

Matrix view: Show the actual weight matrices W_Q, W_K, W_V (small, e.g., 4×4). Show the matmul happening. All numbers visible.

"Break it": Set W_Q = W_K = W_V = Identity. Now there's no learned transformation — attention becomes simple dot-product similarity of raw embeddings. Show that this is much weaker.
Callback: "Remember the soft dictionary from Viz 4.2? Q is the query, K is the key, V is the value. We've just made all three learnable."
Visualization 4.4

Scaling & Softmax: Why √dk Matters

Goal: Show that without scaling, softmax saturates and gradients vanish. A small fix with big impact.
canvas wireframe

Show QK^T scores for a 6-token sequence. With d_k = 64 and no scaling: scores are huge (e.g., 45.2, -38.7, 51.0). After softmax: one value is ~1.0, rest are ~0.0. "The model can only attend to one token — no blending!"

Now divide by √64 = 8. Scores become moderate (5.6, -4.8, 6.4). Softmax gives a smooth distribution. "Now the model can attend to multiple tokens."

Slider: Scale factor from 1 (no scaling) to 16. Watch the attention distribution go from one-hot to uniform as scaling increases. Sweet spot = √d_k.
Visualization 4.5

Multi-Head Attention: Parallel Perspectives

Goal: Multiple heads learn different attention patterns — one for syntax, one for coreference, etc.
canvas wireframe

Grid of attention heatmaps: 1 row per head (2, 4, or 8 heads). Each heatmap shows which tokens attend to which. Color-coded. The patterns are visibly different across heads.

Example: Head 1 focuses on adjacent tokens (local syntax). Head 2 connects "cat" to "was" (long-range agreement). Head 3 attends to punctuation.

Concatenation step: Show all head outputs concatenated, then projected through W_O. "Each head sees a different aspect. The output layer blends them."

Interactive: Num heads slider (1, 2, 4, 8). With 1 head: one pattern must capture everything. With 8: specialization emerges.
Visualization 4.6

Causal Masking: No Peeking at the Future

Goal: For autoregressive generation, position i can only attend to positions ≤ i.
canvas wireframe

Attention matrix with the upper triangle masked (set to -∞). Show: before masking (full attention), after masking (lower-triangular), after softmax (proper causal attention).

"Why?" panel: Show what happens WITHOUT the mask during generation. Token 3 can see token 5 — which is the answer it's trying to predict! "That's cheating. The mask prevents information leakage from the future."

"Break it": Remove the causal mask during training. Training loss drops to ~0 immediately (the model just copies the answer). But generation is garbage. "The model learned to cheat instead of to predict."
Visualization 4.7

The Complete Attention Block

Goal: Assemble everything: multi-head causal attention + LayerNorm + residual connection + FFN.
canvas wireframe

Full block diagram with data flowing through: Input → LayerNorm → Multi-Head Attention → Residual Add → LayerNorm → FFN → Residual Add → Output.

Each component is a box that can be expanded (click to zoom in and see internals) or collapsed. Show concrete tensor shapes at every stage: (batch, seq_len, d_model).

Visualization 5.2

Message Passing: The Core Idea

Goal: Animate one round of message passing. Each node "collects mail" from its neighbors.
canvas wireframe

Animation sequence:

① Highlight one target node v and its neighbors N(v).

② Message creation: Each neighbor sends its feature vector (shown as a colored envelope traveling along the edge toward v). Animate: envelopes slide along edges.

③ Aggregation: All envelopes arrive at v. They stack up. An aggregation function (mean/sum/max) combines them into one vector. Show the math: mean([0.2, 0.8], [0.5, 0.3]) = [0.35, 0.55].

④ Update: The aggregated vector passes through a weight matrix W and activation σ. New feature for v.

Then: repeat for ALL nodes simultaneously. One "round" = one GNN layer.

Layer slider: 1 layer = 1-hop info. 2 layers = 2-hop. 3 layers = 3-hop. Show the receptive field (highlighted subgraph) growing with each layer.

Visualization 5.3

Aggregation Functions: Mean vs Sum vs Max

Goal: Different aggregations have different properties. Sum preserves degree info. Mean is degree-invariant. Max captures salient features.
canvas wireframe

Same graph, three columns — one per aggregation function. Show the resulting node embeddings side by side after 2 layers.

Key experiment: A node with 2 neighbors vs a node with 10 neighbors, all neighbors having the same features. With sum: high-degree node gets 5x larger embedding. With mean: both get same embedding. "Sum is degree-aware. Mean is degree-invariant."

"Break it": Use sum aggregation on a graph with wildly varying degrees (1 to 100). Watch embeddings explode for hub nodes. Fix: normalize by degree (GCN-style).
Visualization 5.4

GCN: Graph Convolutional Network

Goal: The specific message passing rule from Kipf & Welling. Symmetric normalization by degree.
canvas wireframe

The GCN formula: H' = σ(D̂&supmin;½ Â D̂&supmin;½ H W). Break it down step by step:

① Â = A + I (add self-loops). Show: original adjacency → add diagonal → now each node also receives its OWN message.

② D̂&supmin;½ Â D̂&supmin;½ = symmetric normalization. Show: degree matrix D̂, then the normalized adjacency. "This is just weighted mean where the weight depends on both the sender's and receiver's degree."

③ Multiply by features H, then weight matrix W, then activation.

Comparison panel: GCN vs naive message passing (Viz 5.2). Show that GCN's normalization prevents the degree explosion problem from Viz 5.3.
Expert layer: Show spectral interpretation. GCN is a first-order approximation of spectral graph convolutions. The normalized adjacency acts as a low-pass filter on graph signals.

Visualization 7.1

Hard Labels vs Soft Targets: Dark Knowledge

Goal: Show that a teacher's probability distribution contains far more information than the hard one-hot label.
canvas wireframe

Image classification example: A picture of a cat. Hard label: [0, 0, 1, 0, 0] (cat). Teacher's soft prediction: [0.01, 0.15, 0.70, 0.12, 0.02] (cat, but also looks a bit like a dog and a tiger).

Side-by-side bar charts: Hard label = one tall bar. Soft targets = distribution with structure. "The teacher is saying: 'It's a cat, but it's somewhat cat-like AND dog-like AND tiger-like.' That relationship information is the dark knowledge."

Visualization 7.2

KL Divergence: Measuring Distribution Distance

Goal: Build intuition for KL(P||Q). "How different are two probability distributions?"
canvas wireframe

Two editable distributions: P (teacher, blue) and Q (student, red). User drags the bars to change each distribution. KL(P||Q) updates in real-time.

Visual decomposition: KL = ∑ P(x) · log(P(x)/Q(x)). Show each term as a colored bar. When Q(x) is small but P(x) is large, that term is HUGE (heavy penalty for "missing" what the teacher thinks is important).

Asymmetry demo: Show that KL(P||Q) ≠ KL(Q||P). "KL divergence is not symmetric — it matters who is the teacher and who is the student."

Presets: Identical distributions (KL=0), opposite distributions (KL=large), slightly shifted (KL=small).
Expert layer: Show connection to cross-entropy: KL(P||Q) = H(P,Q) - H(P). Since H(P) is constant for a fixed teacher, minimizing KL is equivalent to minimizing cross-entropy. This is why cross-entropy is the standard training loss.
Visualization 7.3

Temperature: Controlling Softness

Goal: Show how temperature softens probability distributions and why this helps distillation.
canvas wireframe

Logits: [5.2, 2.1, -0.5, -1.3, 0.8]. Below: softmax(logits / T) for adjustable T.

Temperature slider: T=0.1 (near one-hot, almost all weight on class 0) to T=1.0 (standard softmax) to T=10.0 (nearly uniform). Animated smooth transition as user drags.

Key insight moment: "At T=1, the dark knowledge is there but hard to learn from — the distribution is too peaked. At T=5, the subtle class relationships become visible. At T=100, everything's uniform — no information at all."

Gradient analysis: Show the magnitude of the gradient from the KL loss at different temperatures. At T=1: gradient is dominated by the top class. At T=5: gradients are spread across classes — the student learns about ALL class relationships.
Callback to Ch 4: "Temperature also appeared in attention softmax (Viz 4.4). Same principle — it controls how 'peaked' or 'spread' the distribution is."
