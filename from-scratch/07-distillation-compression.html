7.1 Hard Labels vs Soft Targets: Dark Knowledge

A teacher's probability distribution carries far more information than a one-hot hard label. The subtle inter-class similarities are the "dark knowledge."

Hard label (one-hot)
Teacher soft prediction
Key Insight: Hard labels say "this is a cat, period." Soft targets say "it's a cat, but it looks a bit like a tiger and a dog." Those inter-class relationships are the dark knowledge.
$$\text{Hard: } [0, 0, 1, 0, 0] \quad\text{vs}\quad \text{Soft: } [0.01, 0.15, 0.70, 0.12, 0.02]$$
Dark Knowledge: The small probabilities assigned to incorrect classes encode structural similarity. A BMW gets confused with a Mercedes, never with a cat.
Why It Matters: A student trained on soft targets learns these relationships and generalizes better than one trained on hard labels alone.
When asked about knowledge distillation, start with dark knowledge: "The teacher's soft probabilities contain inter-class similarity information that hard one-hot labels throw away."

7.2 KL Divergence: Measuring Distribution Distance

How different are two probability distributions? Drag the bars to edit P (teacher) and Q (student), and watch KL divergence update in real-time.

P (teacher)
Q (student)
Per-term contribution
Key Insight: KL divergence measures the information lost when Q approximates P. It heavily penalizes Q for assigning low probability where P assigns high probability.
$$D_{KL}(P \| Q) = \sum_x P(x) \cdot \log\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$$
Asymmetry: \(D_{KL}(P \| Q) \neq D_{KL}(Q \| P)\). It matters who is the teacher and who is the student. In distillation, we minimize KL(teacher || student).
Connection to CE: \(D_{KL}(P \| Q) = H(P,Q) - H(P)\). Since teacher entropy \(H(P)\) is constant, minimizing KL is equivalent to minimizing cross-entropy.
KL divergence is not a true distance (it is asymmetric). When Q puts near-zero probability where P puts mass, KL explodes -- this is why label smoothing helps.
KL(P||Q) penalizes Q for "missing" what P considers important. Think of P as reality and Q as your model -- missing a likely event is far worse than wasting probability on an unlikely one.

7.3 Temperature: Controlling Softness

Temperature scales logits before softmax. Low T sharpens, high T softens. Drag the slider and watch the distribution smoothly transform.

T = 1.0
Raw logits
softmax(logits / T)
Gradient magnitude per class
Callback to Ch 4: Temperature also appeared in the attention mechanism (Viz 4.4). Same principle -- it controls how "peaked" or "spread" the probability distribution is. In attention, low T means sharply focusing on one token; here, low T means one class dominates.
Key Insight: At T=1, soft targets are too peaked for the student to learn subtle class relationships. At moderate T (2-5), the dark knowledge becomes visible. At very high T, everything becomes uniform -- no signal.
$$\text{softmax}(z_i / T) = \frac{e^{z_i/T}}{\sum_j e^{z_j/T}}$$
Low T (0.1): Nearly one-hot. All probability mass on the top class. Gradients only flow through one class.
Moderate T (3-5): Subtle relationships become learnable. Gradients spread across all classes. This is the sweet spot for distillation.
High T (10+): Nearly uniform. No class stands out. All information is washed out.
The T^2 factor in the distillation loss compensates for the reduced gradient magnitude at higher temperatures. Without it, the soft target loss would vanish as T increases.

Key Insight: The distillation loss balances learning from hard labels (ground truth) and soft targets (teacher knowledge).
$$\mathcal{L} = \alpha \cdot \text{CE}(y_{\text{student}}, y_{\text{hard}}) + (1 - \alpha) \cdot T^2 \cdot D_{KL}(\sigma(z_T/T) \| \sigma(z_S/T))$$
Teacher (6 layers, d=256): 2M parameters. High accuracy but slow and large.
Student (2 layers, d=64): 200K parameters (10x smaller). Distilled student retains ~95% of teacher accuracy.
Student from Scratch
Trained on hard labels only. Accuracy: 85%. Misses inter-class relationships.
Distilled Student
Trained with teacher guidance. Accuracy: 94%. Learns dark knowledge about class similarities.
The T^2 scaling factor is crucial. As temperature increases, gradient magnitudes from KL loss decrease by T^2. Multiplying by T^2 keeps the two loss terms on comparable scales regardless of the chosen temperature.

7.5 Quantization: From FP32 to INT8 to INT4

Reduce the precision of weights from 32-bit floats to integers. Watch the smooth distribution become a staircase and track reconstruction error.

Original FP32 weights
Quantized weights
Reconstruction error
Key Insight: Quantization maps continuous float values to a discrete set of integers. Fewer bits means fewer possible values, creating a "staircase" approximation.
$$w_{\text{quant}} = \text{round}\!\left(\frac{w - w_{\min}}{w_{\max} - w_{\min}} \cdot (2^b - 1)\right) \cdot \frac{w_{\max} - w_{\min}}{2^b - 1} + w_{\min}$$
FP32 (4 bytes): Full precision. Baseline. Model size: 100%.
INT8 (1 byte): 4x smaller. Tiny error. 256 discrete levels. Industry standard for inference.
INT4 (0.5 bytes): 8x smaller. Visible staircase. 16 levels. Used in modern LLM serving (GPTQ, AWQ).
INT2 (0.25 bytes): 16x smaller. Only 4 levels. Severe distortion. Research frontier.
Quantization is the single most impactful compression technique for inference cost. INT8 quantization typically loses less than 1% accuracy while cutting memory and compute by 4x.

Pruned weights

Key Insight: Neural networks are heavily over-parameterized. You can remove 90% of weights with minimal accuracy loss -- the network was using a small fraction of its capacity.
$$w_{\text{pruned}} = \begin{cases} w & \text{if } |w| > \theta \\ 0 & \text{if } |w| \leq \theta \end{cases}$$
50% Pruned: Accuracy barely changes. The removed weights were contributing almost nothing.
90% Pruned: Slight accuracy drop (~1-2%). Network is 10x sparser. Huge speedup with sparse matrix ops.
99% Pruned: Noticeable accuracy drop (~5-10%). But the model is 100x sparser.
Lottery Ticket Hypothesis
The Hypothesis
A randomly initialized dense network contains a sparse subnetwork (the "winning ticket") that, when trained in isolation from its original initialization, matches the full network's performance.
Implication
We don't need large networks for their capacity -- we need them to find the right sparse structure. The winning ticket was hiding inside the full network all along.
Pruning works because neural networks are like forests -- most trees (weights) are small and removing them does not change the landscape. The few tall trees carry all the information.

Chapter 7 Payoff: You now understand the full lifecycle: build a large, powerful model (Ch 6), then compress it for deployment (Ch 7). This is exactly how production LLMs are shipped -- a massive teacher model trains a family of smaller, faster student models. Distillation + pruning + quantization: from a data center GPU to your phone.
Key Insight: Each compression technique targets a different axis. Combined, they achieve multiplicative compression.
$$\text{2B params} \xrightarrow{\text{Distill}} \text{200M} \xrightarrow{\text{Prune}} \text{100M} \xrightarrow{\text{INT4}} \text{50MB on disk}$$
Distillation: Reduces architecture size (fewer layers, smaller dimensions). 10x parameter reduction.
Pruning: Removes unnecessary weights within the architecture. 2x additional sparsity.
Quantization: Reduces precision of remaining weights. 4-8x memory reduction.
Production compression pipelines: (1) Train a large teacher on massive data. (2) Distill into a smaller student. (3) Prune the student. (4) Quantize to INT4/INT8 for serving. This is how GPT-4 becomes GPT-4-mini, and how Llama 70B becomes Llama 7B.
Distillation changes the architecture (fewer neurons). Pruning changes the density (fewer connections). Quantization changes the precision (fewer bits). Three independent axes of compression.
